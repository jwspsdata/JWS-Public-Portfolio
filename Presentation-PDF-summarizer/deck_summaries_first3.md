## Bridging the IT/OT divide in Manufacturing: Can AI be the quantum leap we need?

- Source file: [Ariens, David - VanLammeren, Willem.pdf](../../data/raw/pdfs/Ariens%2C%20David%20-%20VanLammeren%2C%20Willem.pdf)
- Author(s): Ariens, David - VanLammeren, Willem (ITOTinsider.com)
- Slide count: 25
- Summary: AI can bridge the IT/OT divide in manufacturing by evolving from a bolted-on tool into an integrated 'Virtual Operator,' but only by respecting the unique constraints of operational technology. The presenters introduce a three-step roadmap - Assistance (GA), Collaboration (Emerging), and Autonomy (far future) - as the path toward that Virtual Operator, distinct from how AI is currently deployed in enterprise IT.
- Chart/table insights: The digital twin architecture diagrams show AI currently sitting outside the core loop of automation, data, and human operators - visually making the case that AI must be embedded within the system rather than added on top.
- Problem: Manufacturing digitalization has stalled for 15 years because OT environments prioritize safety over innovation ('don't explode'), change is expensive, and scaling is linear - making IT-style digital transformation approaches largely ineffective on the factory floor.
- Approach: The presenters propose a structured roadmap that progressively integrates AI into the industrial digital twin ecosystem, moving from assistive tools toward a fully collaborative and eventually autonomous Virtual Operator.
  - Rule 1: Safety before innovation ('don't explode')
  - Rule 2: Acknowledge that change is expensive in OT
  - Rule 3: Account for linear scaling constraints
  - Step 1 - Assistance: AI as Digital Assistant for efficiency
  - Step 2 - Collaboration: Emerging AI integration with operators and engineers
  - Step 3 - Autonomy: Long-term goal of Autonomous/Virtual Operator
- Supporting evidence: The deck contrasts the consumer digital experience ('when I'm home') with the reality of multi-billion dollar production environments still lagging digitally, illustrating the 15-year gap since Industry 4.0 was declared.
- Value add: Teams that follow the roadmap can progressively improve operational efficiency, enable better human-machine collaboration, and ultimately move toward autonomous operations via AI-augmented digital twins.
- Team implications: Operators and engineers must evolve their roles as AI moves from a bolt-on addition to an embedded collaborator within the digital twin, requiring new alignment between IT, OT, operations, and engineering functions.
- Structured lists captured: 3 reasons referenced | Rule items captured: 1, 2, 3

## From Agile 2.0 to "+AI": Telenet's unfiltered journey

- Source file: [Arnst, Barbara - Morel, Johan.pdf](../../data/raw/pdfs/Arnst%2C%20Barbara%20-%20Morel%2C%20Johan.pdf)
- Author(s): Arnst, Barbara - Morel, Johan (Telenet)
- Slide count: 23
- Summary: Telenet shares an unfiltered account of evolving from Agile 1.0 through Agile 2.0 to an AI-integrated operating model, arguing that org design - not technology - is the core blocker to AI adoption. Their distinctive 'Slowify, Simplify, Amplify' framework reframes deliberate pacing and consent-based deployment as prerequisites for sustainable AI transformation, distinct from speed-chasing hype cycles.
- Problem: Telenet's Agile 1.0 'Spotify' model was not wired for outcomes and flow, and adding AI exposed further gaps: fragmented ownership, touchpoint-level rather than end-to-end thinking, and too many stakeholders slowing decision-making.
- Approach: Telenet restructured around customer outcome-oriented tribes (Agile 2.0) and is now layering AI by treating transformation itself as a product with long-lived, accountable teams.
  - Slowify: deliberate pacing before scaling AI
  - Simplify: designing AI team patterns for trust, not just efficiency
  - Amplify: deploying AI with consent, not mandates
  - Treat Transformation as-a-product with skin-in-the-game ownership
  - Organize around E2E customer outcomes via Customer, Platform, and Enterprise tribes
- Supporting evidence: The Billing Experience Tribe example illustrates real AI use cases (agent assist, bill clarification, payment inquiries) alongside concrete challenges: siloed touchpoint focus, contact-avoidance framing, and multi-stakeholder gridlock slowing delivery.
- Value add: Teams gain clearer AI accountability, faster decision-making within tribes, and a roadmap toward E2E workflow ownership (targeting 2026 H2 'Bot' ownership and top 3 E2E workflows).
- Team implications: AI adoption requires reducing naivety about org readiness, shifting leadership mindset toward deliberate slowdown, and embedding feedback loops owned by Enterprise Tribes with long-term accountability rather than finite programs.

## Navigating the AI Evolution

- Source file: [Balakrishnan, Sree - Condo, Chris.pdf](../../data/raw/pdfs/Balakrishnan%2C%20Sree%20-%20Condo%2C%20Chris.pdf)
- Author(s): Balakrishnan, Sree - Condo, Chris (Travelopia, Equal Experts)
- Slide count: 23
- Summary: Travelopia's AI evolution journey shows that structured GenAI integration-not ad hoc experimentation-unlocks exponential productivity gains, reducing delivery cycles from weeks to days. The most distinctive insight is the aspiration to scale from $100M to $500M revenue with the same 200 people by deploying empowered 4-5 member full-lifecycle teams using a two-speed architecture.
- Chart/table insights: Visuals reinforce the trajectory from large traditional squads toward lean 4-5 member teams owning the full build-deploy-maintain-scale lifecycle, with a two-speed architecture separating rapid iteration from stable foundational concerns.
- Problem: Early unstructured GenAI experiments failed, and the team faced heavy skepticism, anxiety about redundancy, and no repeatable framework for integrating AI into software delivery.
- Approach: A structured GenAI-SDLC codified with Equal Experts-combining disciplined planning, specification, and human review with AI-generated code-replaced ad hoc experimentation to make AI adoption repeatable and scalable.
  - Specs -> AI code -> Human review loop
  - Plan -> Spec -> Arch -> Deliver framework
  - Champions-led, one person one project change management
  - Foundation-first, docs as shared language for repeatability
  - Two-speed architecture: fast track iterations + stable track for security/privacy
- Supporting evidence: AI-driven delivery reduced a 3-week manual process to 20 minutes for one module, traditional SDLC of 6-9 weeks dropped to 3 days for the same module, and AI adoption is delivering 40-60% faster execution across Dev, QA, and BA.
- Value add: Teams achieve 40-60% faster execution on key modules, full projects delivered end-to-end through AI, and the organisation aims to scale revenue fivefold without adding headcount.
- Team implications: Squad sizes shrunk from 10-12 specialists to 4-6 cross-functional product engineers, tech leaders had to personally drive cultural change management rather than just tool adoption, and the biggest barrier identified was people, not technology.
